Nuclear Fission




How do fission reactions of U-235 and Pu-239 differ in terms of neutron initiation, energy yield, and radioactive byproducts?
Comparison of Fission Reactions: U-235 vs Pu-239
Factor: Neutron initiation Uranium-235 (U-235): Fissions best with thermal (slow) neutrons Plutonium-239 (Pu-239): Fissions with both thermal and fast neutrons
Factor: Spontaneous fission rate U-235: Very low Pu-239: Much higher — increases risk of premature neutron release
Factor: Energy yield per fission U-235: ~200 MeV Pu-239: ~210 MeV (slightly higher)
Factor: Fission fragments U-235: Common fragments like Cs-137, Sr-90, I-131 Pu-239: Similar fragments, often more neutron-rich
Factor: Actinide production U-235: Minimal (mostly U-236) Pu-239: Significant (e.g., Pu-240, Am-241, Cm-244)
Factor: Alpha emitter formation U-235: Few Pu-239: More, including long-lived transuranics
Factor: Long-lived waste U-235: Less complex and less radiotoxic Pu-239: More radiotoxic and heat-generating
Factor: Reactor suitability U-235: Ideal for thermal reactors (e.g., PWRs, BWRs) Pu-239: Suited for fast reactors and breeder designs
Factor: Weapon design U-235: Easier to use; stable yield Pu-239: More powerful, but harder to handle due to Pu-240 content

What role does U-238 play in nuclear reactors, and how does it lead to the formation of Pu-239?
Although U-238 is not fissile (not directly used in a nuclear chain reaction), it is a fertile component in producing nuclear energy. It is used to absorb neutrons to transform into Pu-239 (a fissile isotope capable of sustaining a nuclear chain reaction).

Consequences:
1. Decay heat Even after shutdown, fission fragments continue to decay and release energy.
~6–7% of total reactor power can persist for hours after shutdown.
This "decay heat" is why cooling systems must operate even when the reactor is off (e.g., Fukushima incident).

2. Radiation damage Neutrons can make surrounding materials (e.g., steel, coolant) radioactive, a process called neutron activation.
This adds to the long-term management of radioactive waste.

3. Biological hazards
Fission fragments like xenon-135 and samarium-149 are strong neutron absorbers ("poisons").
They temporarily reduce reactivity and must be accounted for in reactor control.
